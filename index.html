Chapter 1: Quality Developmental Physical Education

Quality Physical Education (QPE) Defined

QPE helps students increase their:

  • ‚Ä¢ Physical competence
  • ‚Ä¢ Health-related fitness
  • ‚Ä¢ Self-responsibility
  • ‚Ä¢ Enjoyment of physical activity

...so they can be active for a lifetime (p. 6)

‚úÖ Appropriate vs. ‚ùå Inappropriate Practices

‚úÖ Appropriate Practice ‚ùå Inappropriate Practice
Standards-based lessons with clear objectives aligned to developmental status Random activities not connected to standards or development
Instant activities engage all students immediately Students sitting/waiting at start of class
Sequential developmental curriculum One-size-fits-all lessons across grade levels
Positive, emotionally & physically safe environment Activities that single children out or put them "on display"
Maximum participation (50%+ in MVPA) Long lines, elimination games, excessive waiting
Differentiated instruction for all learners Same task for everyone regardless of ability
Formative & summative assessment No assessment or only fitness testing for grades

Chapter 2: The Healthy Child

üìä Physical Activity Guidelines

Children ages 6-17 need 60+ minutes daily of physical activity:

  • ‚Üí Most time in moderate-to-vigorous aerobic activity
  • ‚Üí Vigorous activity at least 3 days/week
  • ‚Üí Muscle-strengthening activity at least 3 days/week
  • ‚Üí Bone-strengthening activity at least 3 days/week

üí™ Health-Related Fitness Components

Component Definition Teaching Implications
Cardiorespiratory Endurance Ability to continue vigorous activity over time without fatigue Use SHORT BURSTS (1-2 min) not long continuous activity; Max HR ~195-205 bpm
Muscular Strength Ability to exert maximum force Activities using BODY WEIGHT: climbing, hanging, swinging, jumping, pushing, pulling
Muscular Endurance Ability to exert force for several repetitions or extended time Weight-bearing activities with SEVERAL REPETITIONS (5-10 inchworms, crab walks, bear walks)
Flexibility Ability of joints to move through full range of motion Stretch AFTER aerobic warm-up; Use static or dynamic (NOT ballistic); Model proper form
Body Composition Proportion of lean mass to fat mass Focus on sound nutrition + daily MVPA + muscular endurance activities

üéØ FITT Principles (for K-2 students)

F - Frequency

How many days/week active

I - Intensity

Heart beating faster/slower? Breathing harder? Muscles tired?

T - Time

Moving for shorter/longer periods

T - Type

Different activities build different fitness components (Physical Activity Pyramid)

‚úÖ Appropriate Fitness Practices

‚úÖ DO:

  • ‚úì Focus on health-related fitness components
  • ‚úì Make fitness FUN and nonthreatening
  • ‚úì Keep results PRIVATE
  • ‚úì Use formative assessment during instruction
  • ‚úì Help students set personal goals
  • ‚úì Make fitness PROGRESSIVE over time
  • ‚úì Promote INTRINSIC motivation

‚ùå DON'T:

  • ‚úó Random mass exercises
  • ‚úó Use fitness tests for grades
  • ‚úó Public display of results or comparisons
  • ‚úó Require long-distance running without conditioning
  • ‚úó Fitness testing without teaching form first

Chapter 3: The Thinking, Feeling, and Socializing Child

üß† Piaget's Cognitive Development Stages

Stage Age Characteristics Teaching Implications
Sensorimotor Birth-2 Movement governed by reflexes; explores world through senses & action Safe environment for crawling/climbing; action-reaction toys; positive feedback
Preoperational 2-7 (Pre-K‚ÄìGrade 2) Uses symbols, elementary logic; egocentric; vivid imagination; asks "why" Simple instructions; pictures on task cards; short activities; station format; stimulate imagination
Concrete Operational 7-11 (Grades 2-5) Logical thinking based on concrete experiences; considers others' perspectives More complex directions (2-3+); longer instruction; can design/modify combinations; apply strategies
Formal Operational 11+ (some 4th-5th) Abstract reasoning; systematic problem-solving; prediction & planning Strategize during gameplay; "what if" scenarios; create complex sequences; mental imagery

üí≠ Self-Concept & Self-Esteem Development

Self-Concept Definition:

Accumulation of knowledge about oneself (beliefs about traits, characteristics, abilities)

Early Childhood:

Concrete - "I am tall!" "I have a dog!" "I play piano!"

Later Childhood:

Abstract - "I am a happy person!" (influenced by peers)

Teachers Foster Positive Self-Concept By:

  • ‚úì Age-appropriate rules with consistent consequences (safety/security)
  • ‚úì Being supportive and encouraging (appreciation/respect)
  • ‚úì Providing inclusive environments where each child succeeds at their level (competence)
  • ‚úì Adequate practice time (learning/competence)
  • ‚úì Providing choice and decision-making (confidence/self-evaluation)

Perceived Competence:

Understanding that one's EFFORT makes the difference in achieving goals

Develops through criterion-referenced goals, NOT social comparison only

üë• Erikson's Psychosocial Development

Stage Age Crisis PE Connection
Initiative vs. Guilt 3-6 (Pre-K) Taking risks, exploring Opportunities to move, explore, succeed = confidence; Criticism/limits = guilt and withdrawal
Industry vs. Inferiority 6-12 (K-5) Producing, learning skills Criterion-referenced goals using personal data = industry; Excessive comparison = inferiority

üéØ Hellison's TPSR Levels

Level Focus Teaching Strategies
Level 0: Irresponsibility Blaming others, denying responsibility Address through levels 1-4
Level 1: Respect Self-control; respecting rights/feelings of others Inclusive environments; conflict resolution; self-officiating
Level 2: Participation Effort and cooperation Limited choice in tasks; redefining success; cooperation activities
Level 3: Self-Direction Working independently without supervision Goal-setting; independent work at stations; personal plans
Level 4: Caring/Leadership Helping others; showing concern Peer assessment; peer teaching; group goals; leadership roles

Empowerment Progression:

Gradually shift decision-making to students: Teacher control ‚Üí Limited choices ‚Üí Independent work ‚Üí Helping/leading others

Always paired with self-reflection on choices and consequences

Chapter 4: The Moving Child

üèÉ Motor Development Phases & Stages

Phase Stage Age Characteristics Teaching Focus
Fundamental Movement Initial ~2-3 First attempts; uncoordinated; limited by body proportions Growth + maturation; enriched play environments; exploration
Emerging-Elementary ~3-5 Transitioning; more control but still awkward Scaled equipment; many practice opportunities; encouragement
Mature ~6-7+ Efficient, coordinated; full range of motion Quality instruction; appropriate equipment; practice + encouragement
Specialized Movement Transition ~7-10 Fundamental skills ‚Üí specialized sport skills Gamelike activities; skill drills; lead-up games (NOT full sports)
Application ~11-13 Skill combinations in cultural activities Modified games/sports; form, precision, accuracy emphasized

üìö Motor Learning Levels (Not age-dependent!)

üî∏ Beginner Level

Characteristics: Inconsistent; uncoordinated; focuses on body placement; can't adapt to changes; can't detect own errors

Goal: Gain greater consistency in performance

Teacher Role: Instruction/feedback about movement pattern, goal of skill, cues in environment, skill variations

üî∏ Intermediate Level

Characteristics: Greater consistency; fewer errors; less conscious attention; beginning to time/adapt; detecting some errors

Goal: Combine skills in fluid sequences; apply skills in various activities

Teacher Role: Refining movement; varying skills; fluidly combining; adapting to authentic, changing environment

üî∏ Advanced Level

Characteristics: Consistent & automatic; effortless appearance; detects/corrects own errors; adapts & anticipates

Goal: Achieve consistent & automatic performance in cultural activities

Teacher Role: Simultaneous performance of multiple skills; strategic decision-making; error detection prompts

üí° Important Connection:

A child in the mature stage of development can still be a beginner learner when first attempting a new skill!

üëÅÔ∏è Perceptual-Motor Development

Visual Perception (affects performance until ~age 12-18):

Visual acuity: Not fully developed until age 10-12; young children are farsighted
Figure-ground: Distinguishing object from background (improves age 4-8) ‚Üí Use bright, contrasting colors
Motion perception: Tracking moving objects (horizontal by 5-6; arc by 8-9) ‚Üí Moderate speeds work best
Depth perception: Detecting distance (matures in teens) ‚Üí Consider ball color, size, speed, trajectory

Kinesthetic Perception:

Body awareness: Recognizing body parts/relationships (most parts ID'd by age 6)
Laterality: Spatial concepts relative to own body; left/right develops age 4-6, mastered by age 10
Directionality: Spatial concepts about objects in environment (develops age 6-12)

üß© Schema Theory (How Skills Are Learned)

Generalized Motor Program (GMP):

Set of rules for performing a skill

Stable Features:

Unchanging sequence of movements (defines the skill)

Example: Step with opposite foot when throwing

Flexible Features:

Vary based on distance, direction, speed, force (defines execution)

Example: Throwing to different distances/targets

Explicit Learning:

Conscious awareness (instruction, demonstrations, feedback)

Implicit Learning:

Unconscious learning through play/exploration

üé® Affordances

Definition:

Flexible features of task/environment that teachers manipulate to fit learner's size and developmental capabilities

Examples:

  • ‚Ä¢ Ball sized to fit child's hand
  • ‚Ä¢ Lower/closer target
  • ‚Ä¢ Slower-moving object
  • ‚Ä¢ Shorter distance
  • ‚Ä¢ Lighter equipment
  • ‚Ä¢ Modified rules
